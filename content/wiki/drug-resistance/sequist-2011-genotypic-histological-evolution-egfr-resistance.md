## Summary

Sequist et al. (2011) present the first systematic prospective serial re-biopsy study of acquired resistance to EGFR tyrosine kinase inhibitors (TKIs) in EGFR-mutant non-small-cell lung cancer (NSCLC). Thirty-seven patients with documented acquired resistance to erlotinib or gefitinib underwent repeat tumor biopsies (97 specimens total across multiple timepoints), enabling comprehensive genotypic and histological characterization of resistance mechanisms. The landmark findings are: (1) EGFR T790M is the most frequent single mechanism (49%), but roughly half of cases involve entirely distinct pathways; (2) MET amplification accounts for 13% of cases and can co-exist with T790M at different tumor sites in the same patient; (3) histological transformation to small-cell lung cancer (SCLC) occurs in 14% of cases, always retaining the original sensitizing EGFR mutation, and these patients respond to etoposide/platinum chemotherapy; (4) epithelial-to-mesenchymal transition (EMT) is a further minor mechanism; (5) multiple resistance mechanisms can co-exist within a single patient owing to spatial and temporal clonal heterogeneity. Longitudinal biopsies demonstrate that the dominant resistant clone can shift after intervening chemotherapy, sometimes allowing re-sensitization to EGFR TKIs. This study established the research re-biopsy paradigm for acquired resistance, provided the mechanistic rationale for third-generation T790M-targeting TKIs (including osimertinib), and revealed lineage plasticity as a clinically significant resistance strategy.

## Key Contributions

1. **Systematic resistance landscape from serial re-biopsy**: The 37-patient prospective cohort with 97 biopsies provided the first comprehensive multi-mechanism prevalence map of EGFR TKI resistance, moving beyond single-case reports to population-level frequencies.

2. **T790M confirmed as dominant but non-exclusive mechanism**: 49% prevalence of T790M at first resistance established it as the primary therapeutic target; the remaining 51% without T790M defined an urgent unmet need for alternative strategies.

3. **MET amplification as a parallel bypass mechanism**: 13% prevalence; critically, 3/5 MET-amplified cases also harbored T790M at a different lesion, demonstrating that resistance is not monoclonal and that combination EGFR+MET inhibition would be needed in a subset of patients.

4. **SCLC histological transformation**: 14% prevalence; the finding that adenocarcinoma can trans-differentiate into SCLC under TKI pressure while retaining EGFR mutations is a paradigm shift for lineage plasticity in targeted therapy resistance. Directly motivated subsequent discovery of RB1/TP53 loss as the mechanistic driver.

5. **Clonal heterogeneity and temporal evolution**: Multi-site and longitudinal biopsies reveal that different resistance mechanisms co-exist spatially and shift temporally — showing that a single biopsy at resistance is insufficient to characterize all clones, and that therapy sequencing can re-shape clonal composition.

6. **Re-treatment sensitivity**: Evidence that intervening chemotherapy can deplete T790M-bearing clones and allow renewed EGFR TKI sensitivity — an early clinical observation underlying later adaptive therapy strategies.

## Methodology

**Cohort and biopsy design**
- 37 EGFR-mutant NSCLC patients meeting Jackman criteria for acquired EGFR TKI resistance; 97 tumor specimens (pre-treatment, first-resistance, post-second-line); core needle biopsies and thoracentesis.

**Molecular profiling**
- SNaPshot/Sequenom multiplexed genotyping for EGFR T790M, exon 19 deletions, L858R, PIK3CA hotspots, BRAF V600E; sensitivity ~5% mutant allele frequency.
- MET amplification by dual-color FISH (MET/CEP7; ratio ≥2.2 or gene copy ≥6 defined amplification).
- Histological review by dedicated thoracic pathologists; SCLC transformation confirmed by neuroendocrine marker IHC (synaptophysin, chromogranin, CD56, TTF-1).

**Functional validation**
- EGFR-mutant cell lines (HCC827, PC9) used to model MET amplification resistance; growth inhibition and signaling (Western blot) with EGFR and MET inhibitors.
- Patient-derived SCLC-transformed cells grown in xenografts; sensitivity to etoposide/cisplatin confirmed in vivo.

## Results

| Resistance Mechanism | Frequency (n=37) | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|
| EGFR T790M | 49% (18/37) | Co-present with sensitizing mutation in cis |
| MET amplification | 13% (5/37) | Can co-occur with T790M at separate sites |
| SCLC transformation | 14% (5/37) | Retains sensitizing EGFR mutation; responds to etoposide/platinum |
| EMT | ~5% | E-cadherin loss; often combined with other mechanisms |
| PIK3CA mutation | ~5% | Always concurrent with T790M |
| Unknown mechanism | ~26% | No defined alteration identified |

**Key quantitative findings**
- T790M detected in 49% of first-resistance biopsies by SNaPshot; 100% of T790M cases retained co-existing sensitizing mutation.
- In patients biopsied at multiple concurrent sites, discordant resistance mechanisms identified between sites in several cases (e.g., T790M at one site, MET amplification at another).
- 4/5 SCLC-transformed patients responded to etoposide-platinum re-treatment.
- In longitudinal tracking, T790M clone proportions decreased after chemotherapy in some patients, enabling renewed erlotinib response.
- HCC827-MET-amp resistant clones: combined erlotinib + MET inhibitor (PHA665752) fully suppressed AKT/ERK signaling and restored growth inhibition; single-agent EGFR or MET inhibition alone was insufficient.
