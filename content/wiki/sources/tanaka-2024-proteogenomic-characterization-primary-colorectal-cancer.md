# CRC Proteogenomics: Primary vs Metastasis (Tanaka 2024)

## Summary

Integrated WGS, RNA-seq, and label-free MS proteomics of 154 primary CRC and 142 liver metastases (340 total samples, including 16 matched primary-metastasis-normal triplets) reveals six proteome-based subtypes: three in primary and three in metastatic tumors. Hypoxia, stemness, and immune signatures define each subtype. Metastatic CRC is enriched for hypoxia signaling, EMT, and suppressed antigen presentation relative to primary tumors. Notably, genomic differences between subtypes are minimal, pointing to proteomic plasticity as the key driver of metastatic adaptation.

## Key points

- Six proteome-based subtypes identified by unsupervised clustering; subtype membership is largely independent of somatic mutation burden
- Hypoxia subtype is enriched in metastases, co-occurring with EMT and metabolic adaptation
- Stemness subtype associates with the ALT pathway and high oncogenic activity
- Immune-cold subtype shows MHC class I/II suppression, most pronounced in liver metastases
- Genomic differences between subtypes are minimal — proteomic, not genomic, plasticity drives metastatic progression

## Project relevance

- Demonstrates the added value of proteomics over genomics for subtype discovery and understanding metastatic biology
- Hypoxia and immune-evasion signatures are relevant to treatment resistance in the drug repurposing project
- Matched primary-metastasis design is a reference for multi-site proteogenomic study design

## Caveats

- Label-free quantification limits precision relative to TMT-based approaches
- Only liver metastases included; other metastatic sites not represented
- Phosphoproteomics not performed; signaling network resolution is limited
