# p53 Loss and Chronic Viral Mimicry Immune Evasion

## Summary

TP53 loss derepresses retrotransposons (LTR, LINE, SINE), initially activating IFN-dependent antiviral responses. Over time, cancer cells develop "viral mimicry conditioning" — increased cytosolic nucleic acid tolerance, dampened IFN signaling via reduced H3K27Ac at ISG promoters, and decreased cellular immunogenicity. This chronic viral mimicry promotes immune evasion from premalignancy through tumor progression. Lamivudine (3TC) partially attenuates this conditioning process.

## Key points

- p53 loss → retrotransposon derepression → chronic viral mimicry tolerance
- IFN signaling dampens over time through epigenetic (H3K27Ac) changes
- "Tolerant" cells resist both TIL-mediated killing and epigenetic therapy-induced viral mimicry
- FDA-approved lamivudine as a potential immunotherapy adjunct

## Project relevance

- Links the most common tumor suppressor mutation (TP53) directly to immune evasion
- Lamivudine repurposing for cancer immunotherapy
- Informs why epigenetic therapy effectiveness may depend on TP53 status
- Early cancer interception target in premalignant lesions

## Caveats

- Focused on ovarian HGSC; generalization to other TP53-mutant cancers needed
- Lamivudine effect is partial, not complete reversal
- Premalignant STIC lesions are clinically difficult to access and treat
