# HBV-PP2A Interaction in HCC (Turnham 2025)

## Summary

Turnham et al. use affinity purification mass spectrometry (AP-MS) to map 145 physical interactions between HBV proteins and the human host proteome in hepatocellular carcinoma. The HBx viral protein is found to remodel PP2A phosphatase complexes by displacing striatin regulatory subunits, leading to rewired kinase signaling. This activates mTOR complex 2, which in turn upregulates YAP. The study argues that HBV contributes to HCC progression through protein interaction rewiring rather than through direct mutagenesis.

## Key points

- 145 HBV-host physical interactions mapped by AP-MS with MiST confidence scoring in HUH7 hepatoma cells.
- HBx displaces striatin subunits from PP2A complexes, rewiring downstream kinase signaling.
- PP2A remodeling activates mTOR complex 2, leading to YAP upregulation.
- HBV-infected HCCs show distinct mutation patterns compared to non-HBV HCCs (Bayesian logistic regression on TCGA LIHC, n=366).
- Network propagation analysis links HBV-interacting proteins to known cancer driver networks.
- mTOR inhibitors identified as a potential therapeutic vulnerability in HBV-associated HCC.

## Project relevance

- Exemplary use of AP-MS-based protein interaction mapping to nominate drug targets — methodology directly applicable to the drug repurposing project.
- Demonstrates how viral remodeling of phosphatase complexes can substitute for somatic mutations in rewiring oncogenic signaling networks.
- mTOR/YAP axis as a potential repurposing target in HBV+ HCC is a concrete example of network medicine target nomination.

## Caveats

- Study is based on in vitro hepatoma cell lines (HUH7); findings need validation in primary HBV-infected HCC tumors.
- AP-MS captures stable interactions; transient or low-affinity interactions may be missed.
- HBV infection model versus actual patient tumor biology may differ in important ways.
