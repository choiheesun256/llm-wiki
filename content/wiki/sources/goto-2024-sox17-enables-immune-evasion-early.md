## Summary

Nature 2024 study showing that transcription factor SOX17 is re-expressed during early colorectal cancer (CRC) tumorigenesis and enables immune evasion. Using AKP (Apc-null/Kras-G12D/Trp53-null) colon organoids with orthotopic transplantation, SOX17 loss reduced tumor engraftment from 80% to 6%. SOX17 suppresses IFNγ signaling in tumor cells, preventing CD8+ T cell-mediated killing, and drives a fetal intestinal programme that generates LGR5- tumor cells with low MHC-I expression. scRNA-seq confirmed that SOX17-null tumors are infiltrated by IFNγ-producing effector-like CD8+ T cells rather than an immunosuppressive microenvironment. MIT/Cold Spring Harbor/Dana-Farber collaboration.

## Key points

- SOX17 is a cell-intrinsic driver of immune evasion, not just a marker — its loss alone is sufficient to restore anti-tumor immunity
- Mechanistically links fetal reprogramming to MHC-I downregulation and IFNγ resistance
- Orthotopic organoid transplantation in immunocompetent mice is a powerful model for studying early immune-tumor dynamics
- scRNA-seq reveals effector CD8+ T cell expansion (not exhaustion) in SOX17-null tumors, suggesting active killing capacity is suppressed by SOX17
- SOX17 induces intrinsic resistance to CD8+ T cell cytotoxicity — relevant to immunotherapy non-response mechanisms

## Project relevance

Directly relevant to drug repurposing in CRC: SOX17 is a transcription factor driving immune evasion via IFNγ pathway suppression. Network medicine approaches could identify upstream regulators or co-dependencies of SOX17 that are druggable. The fetal reprogramming angle is also relevant to understanding tumor cell state transitions and resistance to immune checkpoint therapy.

## Caveats

- Mouse AKP organoid model may not fully recapitulate human CRC biology
- Direct molecular mechanism of SOX17-mediated IFNγ suppression (binding sites, cofactors) not fully resolved
- Clinical validation of SOX17 as a predictor of immunotherapy response in human CRC cohorts is limited in scale
- Species differences between mouse and human immune-tumor interactions must be considered
