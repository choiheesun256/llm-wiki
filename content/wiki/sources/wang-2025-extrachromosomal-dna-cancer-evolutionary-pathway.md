## Summary

bioRxiv 2026. Single-cell multiomics (DNA/RNA/protein) characterization of extrachromosomal DNA (ecDNA) in cancer. Unlike HSR (homogeneously staining regions), ecDNA creates a continuous spectrum of oncogene copy numbers within a tumor. Higher ecDNA dosage leads to proportional increases in transcript levels, chromatin accessibility, protein expression, and cell proliferation. Introduces the concept of "distributive evolution": ecDNA-driven standing phenotypic diversity enables rapid adaptation to selective pressures such as treatment. Cell sorting and time-course experiments demonstrate active restoration of the ecDNA copy number spectrum. UNC Chapel Hill.

## Key points

- ecDNA produces a continuous, not discrete, copy number spectrum — contrasting with HSR states
- Proportional dosage effects across transcriptome, chromatin accessibility, proteome, and proliferation
- "Distributive evolution": population-level copy number diversity as an evolutionary bet-hedging mechanism
- ecDNA spectrum actively recovers after perturbation (cell sorting time-course)
- Single-cell multiomics used to simultaneously profile DNA, RNA, and protein in the same cells

## Project relevance

Relevant to understanding rapid treatment resistance in cancer. The distributive evolution model provides a conceptual framework for why oncogene-amplified cancers are difficult to eradicate. Relevant to drug repurposing contexts where tumor heterogeneity drives resistance. The multiomics methodology is a useful technical reference.

## Caveats

- bioRxiv preprint — not yet peer-reviewed
- Primarily cell line-based; clinical tumor tissue validation needed
- In vivo ecDNA dynamics may differ substantially from cell culture
- Single-cell multiomics technical noise and dropout effects
