## Summary

Molecular Cancer 2025 review. Comprehensive review of autophagy's dual role in tumor immunity. In tumor cells, autophagy modulates antigen presentation, regulates PD-L1 expression, and degrades MHC-I to facilitate immune evasion. In immune cells (T cells, DCs, macrophages), autophagy regulates anti-tumor immune functions. Covers mTOR/AMPK/ULK1/Beclin-1 pathway regulation and therapeutic strategies combining autophagy modulators with immune checkpoint blockade (ICB).

## Key points

- Autophagy-mediated MHC-I degradation as a mechanism of CTL evasion in tumor cells
- PD-L1 stability regulated by autophagy flux — context-dependent stabilization or degradation
- T cell autophagy is required for activation and memory formation
- DC autophagy contributes to antigen cross-presentation
- Macrophage autophagy modulates M1/M2 polarization
- Autophagy inhibitors (chloroquine, hydroxychloroquine) + ICB as a combination strategy

## Project relevance

Relevant background for understanding immune resistance mechanisms in any cancer context. The autophagy–MHC-I–PD-L1 axis is pertinent to drug repurposing efforts targeting immune evasion. The combination therapy rationale (autophagy inhibition + ICB) may inform treatment network analyses.

## Caveats

- Review article — no new experimental data
- Context-dependent (pro- vs. anti-tumor) roles of autophagy complicate therapeutic targeting
- Clinical combination trial data remain limited
- Cancer type– and stage-specific heterogeneity in autophagy roles may be insufficiently addressed
